The Challenge: Understanding CVD Subgroups

The Problem

Cardiovascular Disease (CVD) is complex. Standard predictive models often miss subtle, underlying patient subgroups and unique risk factor patterns.

Key Statistic: Analysis based on a dataset of ~10,000 patient records.

The Goals (Discovery & Validation)

  • Goal 1 (Discovery): Use KModes Clustering (unsupervised) to identify natural patient clusters based on categorical data.
  • Goal 2 (Validation): Compare the clustering insights against the performance of supervised classification models.

From Raw Data to Categorical Features

Clean-up Steps

  • Outlier Removal: Filtered extreme values for height, weight, and blood pressure (2.5th/97.5th percentiles).
  • Correction: Removed records where diastolic BP > systolic BP.

Feature Engineering (KModes Preparation)

  1. Age Binning: Converted continuous age into clinical 5-year intervals.
  2. BMI Class: Calculated and categorized BMI into 6 clinical classes (Normal...Obesity IV).
  3. MAP Status: Calculated Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) and categorized (Low, Normal, High).

Final Output: A dataset composed entirely of numerically encoded categorical features.

Discovery: Two Distinct Patient Subgroups

Methodology

  • Optimal k=2: Clusters confirmed by the Elbow Method.
  • Clustering: KModes algorithm with the robust Huang Initialization.
  • Separate Models: Trained individually for Male and Female populations.

Key Findings

  • Low-Risk Cluster: Clusters 0 (Female) and 2 (Male) consistently showed significantly lower CVD prevalence.
  • Cluster Drivers: Sub-clusters were primarily differentiated by BMI_Class, smoke status, and age_bin.
  • Insight: KModes allows for targeted preventative strategies by profiling the specific risk factors that define each group.

Prediction vs. Explanation: Supervised Model Results

Models Tested: Naive Bayes, Decision Tree, Logistic Regression, Random Forest.

Model Test Accuracy (%) Overfitting Risk Key Finding
Random Forest 72% Low Best Predictive Performance

Conclusion: Random Forest provided the highest predictive accuracy, but KModes provided the essential explanatory power by revealing the underlying structure of the data.

Conclusion and Next Steps

Key Takeaways

  • KModes Success: Provided novel, unsupervised insights into patient segmentation using categorical data.
  • Engineered categorical features proved highly relevant to CVD risk.
  • Model Choice: KModes was superior to $\text{K-Means}$ for this data type.

Future Research

  • Hybrid Clustering: Integrate continuous numerical features (KModes + $\text{K-Means}$).
  • Stability: Explore advanced $\text{KModes}$ initialization methods [2].
  • Robustness: Apply ensemble clustering techniques for more reliable subgroup identification.
